# Lambs and wolves
### Saturday, July 6, 2025
### Austin, TX

I wrote this sermon over a year ago, in July 2025. It is the first, and only, sermon I have ever written. I found it today while writing up some more blog posts--that will be uploaded soon! I'm sorry! I don't have WiFi in my apartment!--and decided to post. Enjoy.

-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Whatever house you enter, first say, 'Peace to this house!' And if anyone is there who
shares in peace, your peace will rest on that person; but if not, it will return to you.

This gospel from Luke starts off simple enough. Jesus asks seventy of his disciples to do him a favor. Go out and map the terrain ahead of me. He is heading to Jerusalem. Some have undoubtedly heard of his miracles, but others will be wholly unfamiliar.
So these disciples are not looking at the mountains, nor the roads and rivers, but the social terrain. Guys, get a vibe check for me. Please.

When I was 16, I moved from New England where I grew up to the South after my dad got a new job. We northerners have not a wonderful impression of the area. I figured everyone here is racist and homophobic and enjoys a weekend of shooting guns and complaining about immigrants. And I went to a tiny wealthy white Christian private school. I am none of those adjectives.

So it was hard for me, for a while. A lot of all of the -isms. People confused me with the only other South Asian girl; she's a year younger and a foot shorter. She was also Pakistani, and I'm Indian, but we came to America to put such petty struggles behind.
I had issues for most of those two years with just about everything, and I did try to adjust. I ran cross country and track, played in jazz band, did science bowl, always topped the class. I'm not invalidating that, but for just a second, imagine what they thought about me.

Southerners have a reputation for being very friendly and kind. They will chat up a
stranger at the grocery store, hug you for no reason. We northerners would rather you
didn't acknowledge us. Don't sit next to me, don't touch me, don't even look at me, and this will all go easier.

So a new kid comes along and doesn't really talk to anyone, goes to class and eats lunch under a tree with a good book. What would you think?

Oh, she's such a [insert slur]. Maybe. Some of them definitely thought that. But most would get offended, upset. I'm trying so hard to be nice and she's not accepting it. Maybe she just doesn't like me. I'll leave her alone.

And that's how I spent my junior year of high school. My closest friend was my AP
chemistry teacher who I'd run cross country with.

Jesus wants his vibe check, even when he already knows that some people will be hostile. He even tells them, "I am sending you out like lambs into the midst of wolves." They don't want the Good News. They want to be left alone on the subway to eat their bodega bagel. (Can you tell I miss New York City?)

And still, he sends his innocent lambs to Jerusalem, to the wolves, in pairs. Whatever is
ahead will be better faced together. And he tells his men to declare peace wherever they go, to whoever they meet. If the people accept the well-wishes, then cure them of their illnesses, be good guests and eat their food. Make the visit worth their while so that when he, the Good Shepherd, comes upon them, they will welcome him with open arms. But if the villagers reject the disciples? No worries, leave them be, Jesus says. Pick yourself up by the bootstraps and move on. And the precious peace you gave to others? It will come right back to you, for whoever rejected you has rejected me. No need to dwell on the matter.

Yes, I identify strongly with the disdainful villagers in this passage. I rejected the peace of my new classmates, of my new city, of my poor school counselor who said some years later, after we became friendly and maybe even friends, that she'd thought I was *scared* of her.

Jesus tells his disciples everything they need to know, to the point of obviousness. Of
course, Lord, we shall be good guests and receive the peoples' offerings with gladness. And still, he continues, preparing them for seemingly every scenario. Is it possible that our Lord Jesus Christ, the Lamb of God, was *scared*?

We have much to worry about in our modern times. Every day, there is a new headline:
floods in Texas have killed 80 people including many sweet little girls at a Christian
summer camp. There is war everywhere. Social programs have been cut drastically, and what will become of our neighbors who need them? Are we scared, dear friends? Do we have a right to be?

There are no big resounding answers to this big resounding suffering. I hope that the Savior is guiding you today the way these disciples were guided before their long journey. But while we do not have step-by-step instructions on how to travel through the 2020s, we do have other things.

I spent most of my first year as the villager, slamming the door in Andrew's or Simon
Peter's face when they asked if I wanted to hear the Good News. But they did not leave!
The disciples -- really, they are people of God -- overwhelmingly stayed. They wiped the
dust of the insult I surely must have inflicted on them and did not explicitly tell, but showed me that the kingdom of God has come near, and gave peace to my house. I was invited to parties and hangouts, to soccer games and group projects. I welcomed them as best as I could. And things got better, slowly and eventually. The peace went to me, and to them, at the same time.

I have enjoyed thinking about this whole gospel, but find most intrigue in the end of it. The seventy disciples came back to Jesus, and they only have good things to share. They say to him, "'Lord, in your name even the demons submit to us!'" (I can't help but think, was I the demon submitting?) And the Almighty was not at all surprised by this, even if one thinks he was a little afraid at sending his followers out so precariously. He said to rejoice instead that their "'names are written in heaven.'" 

So, what can we do in 2025? Send our love into the world just as Jesus sent his disciples out with peace. We can be with each other. Greet a neighbor, ask them if they are okay, and if you can help them. This can be a warm meal, a hot shower, money to help through the hard days, or just your company. Just your laughter and your smile, a hug and your humanity. This is how we get through the 2020s.

And the kingdom of God will come near to you.
